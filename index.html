Why Does the Bridge Wobble? Understanding Resonance

The Story

It was a crisp November morning in 1940. The Tacoma Narrows Bridge in Washington State had been open for only four months. Engineers were proud of its graceful, slender design — at the time, the third-longest suspension bridge in the world. But that morning, something terrifying was about to happen.

A moderate wind of about 42 miles per hour began to blow. This was not unusual for the region. Yet the bridge started to sway — not gently, as suspension bridges sometimes do, but dramatically, twisting and writhing like a living creature. Cars left on the bridge were thrown violently from side to side. A motorist named Leonard Coatsworth had to abandon his car and crawl on hands and knees to the tower.

"The bridge moved in enormous waves. I looked back and my car was curving over the crest of a wave... the whole structure was alive."
— Leonard Coatsworth, November 7, 1940

Within hours, the bridge collapsed into Puget Sound. The only casualty was Coatsworth's dog, Tubby, who refused to leave the car. Engineers, politicians, and the public were shocked. The wind speed was not extraordinary. The bridge was structurally sound by every calculation they had done. What went wrong?

The answer would reshape engineering forever, and it begins with a concept you likely see every day — the rhythm of a child on a swing.

Concept Explanation

Resonance is what happens when an external force applies energy to a system at the system's own natural frequency. Every object has a natural frequency at which it vibrates most efficiently. When the driving force matches this frequency, the amplitude of vibration grows — sometimes catastrophically.

Think of pushing a child on a swing. If you push at random times, the swing barely moves. But if you push each time the swing returns to you — matching its natural rhythm — the swing goes higher and higher with each push, even with very small force. That is resonance.

The Tacoma Narrows Bridge had a natural torsional frequency of around 0.2 Hz. The wind that morning happened to create vortices — swirling air patterns — that struck the bridge at almost exactly that frequency. Each gust added a tiny bit of energy, perfectly timed. The bridge absorbed more and more energy. The oscillations grew beyond what the structure could withstand.

This phenomenon is described mathematically by the resonance condition: when the driving frequency fd equals the natural frequency f0 of the system, amplitude reaches its maximum. In the absence of sufficient damping (energy dissipation), this maximum can be destructive.

Key Points
  • Natural frequency is the frequency at which a system oscillates freely after being disturbed. It depends on the physical properties (mass, elasticity) of the object.
  • Resonance occurs when driving frequency equals natural frequency. At this point, energy transfer is maximally efficient and amplitude grows with each cycle.
  • Damping reduces amplitude by dissipating energy. Real-world systems need sufficient damping to prevent resonance from becoming destructive — hence shock absorbers in cars, dampers in buildings.
  • Practical applications: MRI machines use resonance of hydrogen nuclei. Musical instruments use resonant cavities. Microwave ovens drive water molecules at their resonant frequency (2.45 GHz).
  • Avoiding destructive resonance is critical in structural engineering. Modern bridges use aerodynamic deck profiles, tuned mass dampers, and computer-modeled wind testing.
Exam-Oriented Questions
Board-level Questions — Click to reveal answers
Question 1 — 2 Marks
Define resonance. Under what condition does a forced oscillation exhibit resonance?
Resonance is the phenomenon where a body oscillates with maximum amplitude when the frequency of the applied external force equals the natural frequency of the body. Condition: Driving frequency (f_d) = Natural frequency (f_0). At this point, the system absorbs maximum energy from the driver.
Question 2 — 3 Marks
Explain with an example how resonance can be both useful and destructive. Give one application in each category.
Useful: In MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging), radio waves are tuned to the resonant frequency of hydrogen nuclei in body tissues, enabling detailed imaging. Destructive: The Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940) collapsed because wind vortices matched the bridge's natural torsional frequency, causing runaway oscillations. Modern engineers add dampers to prevent this.
Question 3 — 5 Marks
A child on a swing has a time period of 3 seconds. At what frequency should an adult push the child to achieve resonance? Explain why pushing at twice this frequency is far less effective.
Natural frequency f_0 = 1/T = 1/3 Hz ≈ 0.33 Hz. The adult must push at 0.33 Hz (once every 3 seconds) for resonance. At twice the frequency (0.67 Hz, once every 1.5s), each push does not align with the swing's return phase — sometimes pushing while the swing moves away, which actually removes energy. The system cannot accumulate energy efficiently, so amplitude remains small. This illustrates why phase matching is as important as frequency matching in resonance.

What is the time complexity of Dijkstra's algorithm and why?
Clarity AI · Step-by-step:

Dijkstra's Algorithm Complexity

Step 1 — Setup: We maintain a priority queue (min-heap) of vertices. Initially all distances = infinity except source = 0.

Step 2 — Per vertex: Each vertex is extracted from the heap once — O(V log V) total extractions.

Step 3 — Per edge: Each edge is relaxed at most once — O(E log V) for heap updates.

Result: O((V + E) log V) with a binary min-heap. For dense graphs (E ≈ V²), this becomes O(V² log V).

Simplified Explanation
Imagine you are pushing a friend on a swing. If you push at the exact moment the swing comes back to you every time, your friend goes higher and higher — even with tiny pushes. That is resonance. The bridge had its own natural "swing rhythm," and the wind accidentally matched it perfectly. Every gust added a little more energy, and eventually the bridge swung itself to pieces.
Exam Mode — Resonance
Definition
Resonance is the phenomenon in which a forced oscillation achieves maximum amplitude when the driving frequency equals the natural frequency of the oscillating system.
Key Formula
f_d = f_0 (resonance condition)
Points to Remember
1.Natural frequency depends on mass and elastic properties of the object — it is fixed.
2.At resonance, energy transfer is maximum. Amplitude grows with each cycle (if undamped).
3.Damping reduces amplitude. Insufficient damping leads to structural failure (Tacoma Narrows, 1940).
4.Useful resonance: MRI (hydrogen nuclei), tuned circuits, microwave ovens (2.45 GHz).
5.Harmful resonance: bridge oscillations, building vibrations during earthquakes.
Likely Exam Questions
Q.Define resonance and state the condition for it to occur. (2 marks)
Q.Explain with examples how resonance can be useful and harmful. (3 marks)
Q.Numericals involving period, frequency, and resonance conditions. (5 marks)
